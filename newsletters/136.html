What even is a blockchain?

Last week we got into some of the technical details of the Bitcoin protocol. 21 million maximum supply, halving event, all that jazz. This week I'm going to try and revisit some fundamental concepts of most cryptocurrencies, namely... that they live on datastructures called "Blockchains"

This is a Bitcoin. It is "saying" a Bitcoin wallet address.

Put simply, blockchains are really just a data structure. And to be fair, a relatively slow and inefficient datastructure. They aren't all as slow and inefficient as Bitcoin, but that's because of Bitcoins consensus mechanism but we can talk about consensus and other stuff like that in future newsletters. This episode of flarnchain is meant to just describe the basics, one basics at a time.

In this image, Bitcoin is indicating that it has not reached it's final form.

A blockchain is like a continuously growing vertical tower of blocks. Each block is stacked on top of the previous one and has a connector pointing downwards towards the block beneath it. This connector signifies the relationship and dependency between blocks.

    Individual Blocks: Each block in this tower contains some data, depicted by the counter inside it. As time goes on, new blocks are added to the top of this tower, one after the other.
    Connectors with Arrows: The connectors (lines with arrows) between the blocks are crucial. They represent the "chain" in "blockchain". The arrow at the end of each connector points towards the previous block, signifying that each block, except the very first one, relies on the information from the block below it. This interdependence ensures that once a block has been added, its data can't be changed without altering all the subsequent blocks, making the blockchain very secure.
    Genesis Block: The very first block at the bottom is special and is often referred to as the "Genesis Block". It's the foundation of the entire blockchain. Every other block that gets added above it is linked back through the chain of connectors, all the way to this genesis block.
In essence, the blockchain is a digital record or ledger, where each entry (or block) is connected and secured to the ones before it. The continuous addition of blocks on top signifies the ongoing nature of recording data, and the downward-pointing arrows symbolize the inherent security and connection between these records. The visualization helps in understanding that changing any block would require altering all the blocks above it, making tampering evident and maintaining the integrity of the data.
