The Akash Network

The Akash Blockchain

Akash is a blockchain that is connected to the Cosmos Network. Last week, to dip our toes into the idea of "what is a blockchain", I walked us through the basic concept of a "chain of blocks" of data. A blockchain is a type of data structure that chains together blocks of data. By itself, that's really not that interesting. But combined with the internet, and a robust *consensus engine*, a blockchain becomes something entirely different. It becomes a thing that *exists* on the internet and *has value*, even if a tiny bit.

Sometimes blockchains have a lot of value. Like Bitcoin. The Bitcoin blockchain *has value* and that value could be measured in many ways. One of the ways to measure the value of any *thing that has value* is by looking at it's **Market Capitalization**. The **Market Capitalization** or *market cap* for short of any *thing that has value* is calculated by multiplying the total number of units of **the thing** (it's **Circulating Supply**) by the **Market Price** of one of the units.

For example, right now the **Circulating Supply** of Bitcoin is 19,491,568, and its market price is $27,058.95 (this is as of 9:33pm central time, on September 20, 2023). That means that the **Market Capitalization of Bitcoin** is around $527 million dollars, or roughly **Half A Trillion Dollars**.

What does Akash do?

First we need to sort of distinguish what Akash is again. It's a blockchain, but it's also the native cryptocurrency of the Akash blockchain. A *native cryptocurrency* is one that comes from it's own chain. These are commonly described as **coins** rather than **tokens**. Other good examples of native crypto currencies include **Bitcoin** (BTC) and **Ether** (ETH).

Akash has a bunch of services that it offers it's users. The first and most simple of it's services is that it is a cryptocurrency with several attributes that I personally find interesting:

    It is the native coin of a sovereign blockchain.
    It offers staking rewards to delegators.
    It lives on the cosmos network, thus has a lot of *interoperability* with other blockchains.

Because of these features, I'm already pretty interested in it. But, turns out, thanks to a Software Development Kit called the Cosmos SDK, lots of cryptocurrencies exist that meet the three criteria above. Not all of them have interesting use cases.

*Akash is different* in that regard. It actually does something pretty interesting. Well, it it's native coin can be used to *pay for something* pretty interesting. The coin's primary use on the blockchain, besides security for the network itself, is to pay for cloud computing services.

This sort of setup means that one can use Akash to use a much different type of internet. One that has a totally different type of security mechanism and one that I find very interesting. It's kind of hard to explain, but basically... Akash allows people to use the internet on a completely different type of infrastructure.
